# Brisket Stall Temperature: Why It Happens and When to Wrap

**The stall hits most briskets between 150°F and 170°F internal temperature and can last 2 to 6 hours. It is caused by evaporative cooling as moisture migrates to the surface of the meat and evaporates faster than your fire adds heat. Wrapping speeds you through it, but it is not required. Many pitmasters ride it out bare and get better bark for it.**

---

## What temperature does the brisket stall actually happen at?

Most briskets stall somewhere between 150°F and 170°F, with 160°F to 165°F being the most common range. The exact number shifts based on your pit temperature, humidity, airflow, and how much fat and moisture the particular brisket is carrying. Do not treat any single number as gospel.

A brisket cooked at 225°F will often stall lower and longer than one cooked at 275°F. Higher pit temps drive more aggressive evaporation, but they also push more heat into the meat faster, so the stall tends to be shorter and can occur slightly higher in the internal temp range. [Food scientist Greg Blonder documented this evaporative cooling mechanism in detail for AmazingRibs.com](https://amazingribs.com/more-technique-and-science/more-cooking-science/the-stall-brisket-stall-pork-butt-stall-and-what-to-do/), showing that the stall is essentially your brisket acting like a sweating athlete, shedding heat as fast as your smoker adds it.

The practical takeaway: stop checking the clock and start trusting your thermometer. A brisket that has been at 163°F for three hours is not broken. It is doing exactly what physics says it should.

---

## Why does the stall last so long on some briskets and not others?

Duration comes down to surface area, fat content, and your cooking environment. A full packer brisket with a thick fat cap has more surface area and more intramuscular moisture to lose than a trimmed flat. That means more evaporative cooling, which means a longer stall.

Pit humidity matters just as much as pit temperature. A tight offset with little airflow holds more ambient humidity than a wide-open barrel cooker. Higher humidity around the meat slows evaporation, which can actually shorten the stall because the cooling effect is less aggressive. Conversely, a pellet grill running a convection fan at 225°F moves dry air across the brisket constantly, which can stretch the stall out to 5 or 6 hours on a heavy packer.

If you are cooking in cold, dry weather, expect a longer stall. If you are cooking in a humid Texas summer, it may blow through faster. These are not excuses, they are variables you can account for once you understand the mechanism.

## Is the stall hurting my brisket?

No. The stall is not damaging the meat. It is actually doing useful work.

During those hours in the 150°F to 170°F window, collagen is converting to gelatin. That conversion is what gives a properly cooked brisket its unctuous, sliceable texture rather than a dry, fibrous chew. [According to food science writer Harold McGee in *On Food and Cooking*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/On-Food-and-Cooking/Harold-McGee/9780684800011), collagen begins converting to gelatin around 160°F and the process accelerates with time at temperature. The stall is essentially buying you that time at the right temperature range for collagen breakdown to happen.

Rushing through the stall by cranking your pit to 325°F or higher can push the internal temp past the collagen conversion window too fast, leaving you with tight, dry muscle fibers before the connective tissue has fully broken down. That is how you get a brisket that probes tight and slices like pot roast.

## Should you wrap during the stall or push through?

This is the actual decision point, and there is no universal right answer. Wrapping in butcher paper or foil will shorten your cook by 1 to 3 hours. Riding it out bare will give you harder, darker bark and a more pronounced smoke ring. Both approaches produce excellent brisket in the right hands.

The Texas crutch (foil wrap) is effective because foil traps moisture and raises the humidity immediately around the meat, which eliminates the evaporative cooling effect and lets pit heat drive the internal temp up again. Butcher paper is more breathable, so it moderates the stall rather than eliminating it, and it protects bark better than foil.

Competition pitmasters are split. Teams cooking KCBS-style often wrap in foil with tallow or butter to control texture and timing. Central Texas-style pitmasters like those at [Franklin Barbecue](https://franklinbbq.com/) have made unwrapped or butcher paper-wrapped brisket a calling card. Aaron Franklin himself has described the paper wrap as a way to protect bark without steaming the meat the way foil does.

If you are on a deadline, wrap in butcher paper when the brisket hits 165°F to 170°F internal. If you have time and want maximum bark development, leave it bare and let the stall run. Either way, you are not done when you hit 200°F or 203°F on the thermometer. You are done when the probe slides into the flat with no resistance, like pushing into warm butter. That might be 198°F on one brisket and 210°F on another.

## How do you know when the stall is actually over?

Your thermometer will start moving again, usually slowly at first, then more steadily. After the stall breaks, most briskets climb at roughly 1°F to 2°F per 10 to 15 minutes at 225°F to 250°F pit temp. If you have been stuck at 163°F for four hours and suddenly see 165°F, then 167°F over the next 30 minutes, the stall is breaking.

Do not pull the brisket the moment the stall ends. The real finish line is probe tenderness in the thickest part of the flat, not a number on a screen. The point will always probe tender first because of its higher fat content. The flat is where briskets fail or succeed. Probe there, and keep cooking until it tells you it is ready.

## Frequently asked questions

### What temperature does the brisket stall happen at?
The brisket stall typically occurs between 150°F and 170°F (65°C–77°C). At this range, evaporative cooling from moisture escaping the meat's surface counteracts the heat from your smoker, causing the internal temperature to plateau for anywhere from 2 to 6 hours. It's a completely normal part of the smoking process and not a sign that something has gone wrong with your cook.

### How long does the brisket stall last?
The stall can last anywhere from 2 to 6 hours, depending on the size of your brisket, smoker temperature, and humidity levels. Larger packer briskets tend to stall longer than trimmed flats. Cooking at higher smoker temperatures (275°F–300°F) can shorten the stall, while lower-and-slower cooks at 225°F may extend it significantly. Patience is key—rushing through the stall often leads to tough, undercooked brisket.

### Should I wrap my brisket during the stall?
Wrapping your brisket during the stall is one of the most effective ways to push through it faster. Using butcher paper or aluminum foil (the Texas Crutch method) traps heat and reduces evaporative cooling, shortening cook time by 1–3 hours. Foil retains more moisture but softens the bark, while butcher paper preserves bark texture better. Most pitmasters wrap when the brisket hits 160°F–170°F and the bark is fully set.

### Can I just wait out the brisket stall without wrapping?
Yes, you can absolutely cook through the stall without wrapping, and many competition pitmasters prefer this approach for a firmer, drier bark. Expect the stall to last several hours longer, and ensure your smoker maintains a consistent temperature throughout. Cooking unwrapped at 250°F–275°F helps move things along without sacrificing bark quality. The trade-off is a longer total cook time, so plan accordingly and avoid time pressure when going unwrapped.

### Does the stall happen on a pellet grill the same way it does on an offset smoker?
Yes, the brisket stall occurs on pellet grills just as it does on offset smokers because it's caused by the meat itself, not the cooker. Evaporative cooling happens regardless of your heat source. However, pellet grills tend to have higher ambient humidity inside the cooking chamber, which can sometimes prolong the stall slightly. The wrapping strategies and target temperatures remain the same across both cooker types.
