# How Long to Smoke a Brisket?

**The short answer:** A whole packer brisket at 225-250°F takes 12-18 hours. At 275°F, plan for 8-12 hours. Weight matters less than thickness and fat cap. The only finish line that counts is probe tender, not a clock or a single internal temp. Use time as a planning tool, not a done signal.

---

## Why Does Your Brisket Time Keep Coming Out Wrong?

You planned for 14 hours. It hit 203°F at hour 10. You pulled it, sliced it, and the flat was dry and crumbly. Or the opposite: your guests were sitting down and the brisket was still at 185°F with no end in sight.

This is the most common brisket problem. And it almost always comes from treating time and temperature as fixed rules instead of rough guides.

Brisket does not cook on a schedule. It cooks on collagen. The pectoralis major muscle is loaded with connective tissue. That tissue has to break down into gelatin before the brisket is done. That process does not happen at a specific time. It happens at a specific condition, and that condition is probe tender.

Until you feel that probe slide in like warm butter, the brisket is not done. A timer cannot tell you that.

---

## What Temperature Should You Actually Smoke a Brisket At?

Most pitmasters run 225-275°F. The sweet spot for most cooks is 250°F.

At 225°F you get more smoke absorption and a longer cook window. That window gives you more control, but it also means more time managing your fire. At 275°F the cook moves faster and you still get great bark. Many competition cooks run 275°F or higher to hit tight timelines.

Here is what that looks like in real time:

- **225°F:** Roughly 1.5 to 2 hours per pound on a whole packer
- **250°F:** Roughly 1 to 1.5 hours per pound
- **275°F:** Roughly 45 minutes to 1 hour per pound

These are estimates. A 14-pound brisket at 250°F might take 16 hours. It might take 13. Thickness of the flat, fat cap depth, your actual pit temp versus your dial temp, and humidity all push that number around.

Never trust your smoker's built-in thermometer. Use a quality leave-in probe thermometer placed in the thickest part of the flat, away from fat seams.

---

## What Is the Difference Between USDA Safe and Actually Done?

The USDA says beef is safe at 145°F. For brisket, that number is almost useless.

At 145°F, a brisket is technically safe to eat. It is also tough, chewy, and nowhere close to what you are going for. The collagen has not converted. The fat has not rendered. You would be better off braising it at that point.

The internal temp range where brisket becomes edible BBQ is 195-210°F. Most briskets hit probe tender somewhere in that window, usually between 200-205°F. But some briskets need to hit 210°F before the probe slides clean. Others are ready at 197°F.

This is why probe tender is the real target. You are not chasing a number. You are chasing a texture.

Use a thin probe or an instant-read thermometer and poke it into the thickest part of the flat. If it goes in with little to no resistance, like pushing into room-temperature butter, the brisket is done. If you feel any drag or tug, give it more time.

Check every 30 minutes once you hit 190°F internal.

## What Is the Stall and How Long Will It Last?

The stall is real and it will test your patience.

Somewhere between 150-170°F internal temp, your brisket will stop climbing. It might sit there for 2, 3, even 5 hours. This is not your smoker losing heat. It is evaporative cooling. Moisture leaving the surface of the meat cools it at the same rate the smoker is heating it.

The fix is simple: wait it out or wrap it.

If you wrap in butcher paper or foil, the stall shortens because you trap moisture and stop the evaporation. Many competition pitmasters wrap in butcher paper around 165-170°F to push through the stall while still allowing some bark development.

But wrapping is not required. Plenty of serious pitmasters run naked briskets from start to finish. The stall lasts longer, but the bark is harder and the smoke ring is often more pronounced.

If you are on a time crunch, wrap. If you have the time and want maximum bark, push through naked.

One thing to never do: crank your pit temp to 325°F to break the stall faster. You will tighten the muscle fibers before the collagen has time to convert. The flat will dry out. The point might be okay, but you will ruin the slice.

## How Does Brisket Size Affect Cook Time?

A 12-pound brisket and a 16-pound brisket do not just differ by 4 pounds. They often differ by 3-4 hours of cook time.

But raw weight is not the only factor. Thickness matters more than weight. A 14-pound brisket that is thick through the flat will take longer than a 16-pound brisket that is wide but thin.

When you are buying a brisket, look at the flat. You want it at least 1 inch thick at the thinnest point. Thin flats overcook fast. By the time the point is probe tender, the flat is already dry.

For planning purposes, use this framework:

- **10-12 lb brisket at 250°F:** Budget 12-14 hours
- **13-15 lb brisket at 250°F:** Budget 14-17 hours
- **16+ lb brisket at 250°F:** Budget 16-20 hours

Always build in a 2-hour rest buffer. More on that below.

---

## Does the Type of Smoker Change Cook Time?

Yes, and it matters more than most people account for.

Offset smokers run with convection heat. The heat moves across the brisket. Fat side placement, where you position the brisket relative to the firebox, and how well you manage your fire all affect cook time and evenness.

Pellet grills run with a fan-forced convection system. They tend to run slightly more efficient at a given temp setting than a traditional offset. A brisket at 250°F on a pellet grill often cooks a bit faster than the same brisket at 250°F on an offset.

Kamado-style cookers hold moisture well and run efficient. They can shorten cook times too.

Cabinet smokers and insulated vertical smokers (like an insulated reverse flow) are extremely efficient. Cooks can run 30-45 minutes faster per pound compared to a leaky stick burner.

The point is this: your friend's cook time on their offset does not automatically translate to your pellet grill. Learn your specific cooker. Track your cooks with a log. After 5-6 briskets, you will have a real baseline for your equipment.

## How Long Should You Rest a Brisket?

At minimum, 1 hour. Ideally, 2-4 hours.

Resting is not optional. It is part of the cook. When you pull a brisket off the smoker, the muscle fibers are tight and the juices are pushed to the center. If you slice right away, those juices run out onto your cutting board. You lose moisture and flavor.

During a rest, the fibers relax and reabsorb juice. The internal temp also continues to rise slightly, usually 5-10 degrees, which can push a brisket that was borderline probe tender over the finish line.

Wrap the brisket in butcher paper or foil, then wrap that in a towel, and place it in a dry cooler. This is called a faux cambro. A good cooler will hold a brisket safely above 140°F for 4-6 hours.

Competition cooks often hold briskets this way for 4+ hours before service. The rest does not hurt the brisket. It almost always helps it.

---

## How Do You Plan a Brisket Cook From Start to Finish?

Work backward from your serve time.

Say you want to serve at 6 PM. You need at least a 2-hour rest. So the brisket needs to be off the smoker by 4 PM at the latest.

You have a 14-pound brisket. At 250°F, budget 15 hours. That means you need to be on the smoker by 1 AM.

Most pitmasters do one of two things. They either start the night before and let it rest in a cooler through the morning. Or they start very early in the morning and plan for a long rest if it finishes early.

Early is always better than late. A brisket that finishes 3 hours early and rests in a cooler is still a great brisket. A brisket that is not done when guests arrive is a problem with no good solution.

Build your schedule around this order:

1. Estimated cook time (use weight and pit temp to estimate)
2. Add 2 hours of stall buffer
3. Add 2-4 hours of rest time
4. Count backward from serve time to get your start time

If that puts your start time at 11 PM, start at 11 PM. Brisket does not care about your sleep schedule.

## The One Mistake That Wastes All Your Work

You did everything right. Good trim. Good rub. Good fire management. You hit probe tender. Then you sliced it too early or too thin and the flat fell apart.

The flat needs to be sliced against the grain. The point and the flat run in different directions. If you are not separating them and slicing each against its own grain, you are leaving texture on the table.

Slice the flat at about a pencil-width thickness. The point can go thicker since it has more fat and forgives a little more. Burnt ends come from the point. Slices come from the flat.

Get the rest right, get the slice right, and the time question mostly takes care of itself. The clock is just a planning tool. Probe tender is the answer.

## Frequently asked questions

### How long does it take to smoke a brisket?
A whole packer brisket typically takes 12–18 hours to smoke at 225–250°F, though timing varies based on size, fat content, and your smoker. Plan roughly 1 to 1.5 hours per pound as a general guideline. Always cook to internal temperature rather than time alone, brisket is done when it reaches 200–205°F and probes like soft butter. Build in a 1–2 hour rest period before slicing to let juices redistribute.

### What temperature should I smoke a brisket at?
Most pitmasters smoke brisket between 225°F and 275°F. The 225–250°F range produces deeply smoky, tender results but requires more time, while 275°F speeds up the cook without sacrificing much quality, a popular choice for competition BBQ. Avoid exceeding 300°F, as the exterior can dry out before the collagen fully breaks down. Consistent temperature control matters more than hitting an exact number, making well-tuned pellet grills and offset smokers ideal for brisket cooks.

### When should I wrap my brisket during the smoke?
Wrap your brisket when the internal temperature stalls, typically between 150–170°F, which usually occurs 6–8 hours into the cook. Wrapping in butcher paper (the "Texas crutch" method) helps push through the stall while preserving a firmer bark compared to aluminum foil. Foil wrapping retains more moisture and speeds up cooking but can soften the crust. Some competition BBQ cooks skip wrapping entirely for maximum bark development, accepting a longer overall cook time.

### How do I know when a smoked brisket is done?
Brisket is done when it reaches an internal temperature of 200–205°F and a probe or toothpick slides into the flat with zero resistance, often described as probing like warm butter. Temperature alone isn't enough; a brisket at 200°F can still be tough if the collagen hasn't fully rendered. Always probe the thickest part of the flat, not the point. After pulling it off the smoker, rest the brisket for at least one hour before slicing.

### How much brisket should I buy per person?
Plan on ½ to ¾ pound of raw brisket per person after accounting for the significant weight loss during smoking, a whole packer brisket typically loses 30–40% of its weight through fat rendering and moisture evaporation. For a dinner party of 10, a 15–18 lb packer brisket is a safe starting point. If serving alongside heavy sides like mac and cheese or pork ribs, you can lean toward the lower end of that estimate.
