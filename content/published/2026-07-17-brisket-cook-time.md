# Brisket Cook Time: How to Pick the Right Method for Your Schedule

**Quick answer:** Plan on 60 to 90 minutes per pound at 225-250°F, or 45-60 minutes per pound if you run hot at 275-300°F. A 13-pound packer takes roughly 12-16 hours low and slow, or 8-10 hours running hot. Probe tenderness always wins over the clock. Build in a 2-3 hour buffer no matter which method you pick.

## How Long Does a Brisket Really Take?

There's no single number. A 12-pound packer can take anywhere from 10 to 18 hours depending on your pit temp, the meat itself, and the weather outside. Anyone who gives you one fixed number is guessing.

Time depends on four things: cook temp, brisket weight, how much the point and flat vary in thickness, and outside conditions like wind and cold. Two briskets from the same cow can cook at different speeds. That's why pitmasters go by internal temp and probe feel, not a timer.

Here's the general math competition cooks and backyard smokers both rely on:

- **225-250°F (low and slow):** 60-90 minutes per pound
- **275-300°F (hot and fast):** 45-60 minutes per pound
- **300-325°F (aggressive hot and fast):** 35-45 minutes per pound

These are starting points, not promises. Your smoker, your brisket, and your weather will push the number around.

## Should I Cook Low and Slow or Hot and Fast?

Low and slow gives you a bigger margin for error and a more traditional bark and smoke ring. Hot and fast gets you done in half the time with nearly the same result if you know what you're doing. Pick based on your schedule and your comfort level, not tradition alone.

### Low and Slow (225-250°F)

This is the method most pitmasters learn first. You smoke at 225-250°F until the brisket hits 195-205°F internal and probes like butter.

**Time estimate:** 60-90 minutes per pound. A 13-pound packer takes 12-16 hours plus rest.

**Why pick it:**
- More forgiving. Small temp swings don't wreck the cook.
- Deeper smoke penetration and a thicker bark.
- Easier to manage overnight on an offset since the fire burns slower.

**Downside:**
- Long cook. You're looking at an overnight session for a Saturday lunch serve.
- More time for the stall to eat into your schedule.
- Requires more fuel and more tending on a stick burner.

### Hot and Fast (275-300°F)

Plenty of competition teams run hot and fast because it frees up time without sacrificing quality. You smoke at 275-300°F until the brisket hits the same 195-205°F range and probes tender.

**Time estimate:** 45-60 minutes per pound. That same 13-pound packer finishes in 8-10 hours.

**Why pick it:**
- Cuts your cook time nearly in half.
- Less overnight tending. You can start at 6 a.m. and pull dinner by 4 p.m.
- Renders fat just as well if you manage your temps right.

**Downside:**
- Less margin for error. Temp spikes hurt more when you're already running hot.
- Bark can form faster than the inside renders, so you need to watch it closely in the last two hours.
- Some pitmasters feel the smoke flavor is lighter, though many blind taste tests say otherwise.

## Which Method Fits My Schedule Better?

If you need brisket ready by a specific hour and can't babysit an overnight fire, hot and fast is the safer bet. If you've got the time and want maximum smoke flavor, low and slow is worth the extra hours.

Think backward from your serving time. Say you want to eat at 6 p.m. Pick your method, then add a 2-3 hour rest and a 2-hour buffer for stalls or plateaus.

**Low and slow math for a 13-lb brisket, 6 p.m. serve:**
- Cook time: 14 hours
- Rest time: 2 hours
- Buffer: 2 hours
- Start time: 2 a.m.

**Hot and fast math for the same brisket:**
- Cook time: 9 hours
- Rest time: 1.5 hours
- Buffer: 1.5 hours
- Start time: 8 a.m.

That's the real decision. Low and slow means a middle-of-the-night start or an overnight cook you monitor in shifts. Hot and fast means you can start after breakfast and still hit dinner.

## What About the Stall? Does It Change My Timeline?

Yes. Almost every brisket hits a stall between 150-170°F internal, and it can sit there for one to three hours. Build that time into your plan instead of panicking when the temp stops climbing.

The stall happens because moisture evaporating off the surface of the meat cools it down, canceling out the heat coming from your pit. It's normal. It's not a sign anything is wrong.

Low and slow cooks feel the stall more because the whole cook is already long. A three-hour stall in a 14-hour cook is a bigger relative delay than a three-hour stall in a 9-hour hot and fast cook, but it still happens in both.

Wrapping in butcher paper or foil can push through the stall faster by trapping moisture. This is optional. Plenty of competition pitmasters run the whole cook unwrapped and just accept the extra time. If your schedule is tight, wrapping at 165°F internal can save you 1-2 hours. If you've got time to spare, skip it and let the bark set naturally.

## Should I Trust the Clock or the Thermometer?

Trust your thermometer and probe feel, not the clock. The USDA minimum of 145°F is just a safety guideline; brisket needs to reach around 203°F for true doneness. Plan your cook time based on weight and desired tenderness.


Trust the thermometer and the probe feel every time. The clock only exists to help you plan your day. It should never tell you when the brisket is done.

The USDA minimum safe temp for beef is 145°F. That number is about food safety, not doneness. Brisket at 145°F is tough, chewy, and nowhere near ready to eat. Pitmasters cook well past that minimum because collagen and connective tissue in a brisket don't break down until the internal temp reaches 195-205°F.

Here's how to check for real doneness:
- Insert a probe or instant-read thermometer into the thickest part of the flat.
- It should slide in with almost no resistance, like pushing a probe into softened butter.
- Check multiple spots. The point and flat cook at different rates, so one might read tender before the other.
- If you feel any tightness or resistance, give it more time regardless of what the number says.

Some briskets probe tender at 195°F. Others need to go to 205°F or even 208°F. Weight and time estimates get you in the ballpark. The probe tells you the truth.

## What If I'm Cooking for a Specific Event?

Cook backward from your event time, adding extra buffer. A rested brisket can hold safely, so aim to finish early. For example, if serving at 5 p.m., start cooking well before dawn and plan for rest periods.


Cook backward from your event time and always add a padding window. A rested, wrapped brisket holds safely for hours, so finishing early beats finishing late every time.

Here's a simple planning method:

1. **Pick your serve time.** Say it's 5 p.m. for a backyard cookout.
2. **Decide your method.** Low and slow or hot and fast.
3. **Calculate cook time based on weight.** A 14-lb packer at low and slow could take 16 hours.
4. **Add 2 hours for stall and variance.**
5. **Add rest time.** Minimum 1 hour, ideally 2-3 hours wrapped in a towel inside a cooler.
6. **Set your start time and add another hour of cushion.**

For that 14-pound brisket serving at 5 p.m., you're looking at a start time around 9 p.m. the night before if you want real cushion. That sounds extreme, but it's exactly why so many pitmasters start briskets at night and sleep near the pit, or run pellet grills with WiFi controllers that alert them if temps drop.

If overnight cooking doesn't work for your setup, hot and fast solves the problem. Starting that same 14-pound brisket at 6 a.m. and running 300°F gets you done by 3 or 4 p.m., leaving plenty of rest time before a 5 p.m. serve.

## Does Holding and Resting Change My Total Time?

Yes, and skipping this step is one of the biggest mistakes new cooks make. Always plan at least 1 hour of rest, and 2-4 hours if you can manage it.

Resting lets the juices redistribute through the meat. Cut too early and juice runs out onto the cutting board instead of staying in the meat. A brisket pulled straight off the smoker at 203°F and rested for 3 hours in a cooler wrapped in towels will slice cleaner and taste juicier than one sliced immediately.

Long holds also give you flexibility. If your brisket finishes 2 hours early, that's not a problem. Wrap it in towels, drop it in a dry cooler, and it will hold safely at serving temp for hours. This is actually the preferred outcome for competition teams, since a rested brisket is easier to slice consistently for turn-in.

Finishing late is the real problem. That's why the buffer time matters more than people expect. Build in the extra hours upfront so a slow cook doesn't wreck your dinner plans.

## Quick Decision Checklist

For tight schedules, go hot and fast (275-300°F). For all-night smokes, low and slow (225-250°F) yields better bark. Always start early to account for rest time; late brisket ruins the event.


Use this to pick your approach fast:

- **Tight on time, need it done in a workday?** Run hot and fast at 275-300°F.
- **Have all night and want maximum bark and smoke?** Run low and slow at 225-250°F.
- **Cooking for a strict event time?** Start earlier than you think you need to. Rested brisket holds well; late brisket ruins dinner.
- **New to brisket?** Low and slow gives you more room to recover from mistakes.
- **Comfortable managing pit temps closely?** Hot and fast saves hours with no real tradeoff in quality.
- **Unsure if it's done?** Ignore the clock. Probe the flat. If it's not butter soft, it's not done.

Cook time is a planning tool, not a rulebook. The brisket finishes when it's tender, not when the timer says so.

## Frequently asked questions

### How long does it take to smoke a brisket per pound?
Plan on roughly 1 to 1.5 hours per pound when smoking at 225-250°F. A typical 12-14 pound packer brisket will take 12-18 hours total, though this varies based on the meat's thickness, fat content, and how consistently your smoker holds temperature. Rather than relying solely on time, use it as a planning guide and track internal temperature closely as the cook progresses, since brisket often hits stalls that can add hours unpredictably.

### What internal temperature should brisket be when it's done?
Brisket is done between 200-205°F internally, but temperature alone isn't the full story. The real test is probe tenderness, a thermometer probe should slide into the thickest part of the flat with little resistance, like pushing into softened butter. Some briskets finish as low as 195°F while others need 208°F. Pull it based on feel, then rest it for at least 45 minutes to an hour before slicing for the best texture.

### Why is my brisket taking so much longer than expected?
The stall is the most common culprit, where evaporative cooling stalls internal temp at 150-170°F for hours. Other factors include cooking below your target pit temperature, opening the lid frequently, a particularly thick or cold brisket, or humidity and wind affecting your smoker's efficiency. Wrapping in butcher paper or foil (the "Texas crutch") once you hit the stall can push through it faster and keep your overall cook time more predictable.

### Should I cook brisket at 225 or 250 degrees?
Both work, but 250°F shaves 2-4 hours off your cook time compared to 225°F without sacrificing much tenderness. Lower temperatures give more margin for error and are often preferred by competition cooks chasing maximum smoke ring and bark development. For home cooks with time constraints, 250-275°F produces excellent results while reducing the risk of running out of daylight or fuel on long overnight cooks.

### How long should brisket rest after smoking?
Rest brisket for a minimum of 45 minutes, though 1-2 hours wrapped in a towel inside a dry cooler is ideal. This resting period allows juices to redistribute throughout the meat and lets residual heat finish carryover cooking. Skipping the rest or cutting it short results in juices spilling out onto the cutting board instead of staying in the meat, producing a drier, less flavorful slice.
